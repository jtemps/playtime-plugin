# Collaborative & Proactive Solutions (Ross Greene)

**Core idea**: **Kids do well if they can.** Challenging behavior is the result of lagging skills + unsolved problems. The solution isn't reward/punishment — it's identifying the lagging skills and solving problems collaboratively, *with* the kid.

## Pillars

- **"Kids do well if they can"** (vs. "kids do well if they want to"): behavior is signal, not a moral failing
- **Lagging skills**: frustration tolerance, flexibility, problem-solving, emotion regulation, etc. — these are skills, and they can be taught
- **Unsolved problems (UPs)**: specific recurring situations that go badly — identify these, work them one at a time
- **Plan B (Collaborative Problem-Solving)**: a 3-step conversation
  1. **Empathy step**: "I've noticed that [unsolved problem]. What's up?" → genuinely listen, repeat back
  2. **Define the problem**: share your concern, neutrally
  3. **Invitation**: "I wonder if there's a way we could solve this so it works for both of us. Got any ideas?" — kid goes first
- **No Plan A (impose) or Plan C (drop)** as defaults; use Plan B
- **Proactive, not reactive**: solve UPs in calm moments, not in the heat

## Design implications for packets

- **Lagging-skills awareness**: parent footer occasionally names a relevant skill ("today's activity gives [Kid] practice with frustration tolerance")
- **Plan B scripts**: when the parent footer mentions a tough moment, offer the 3-step CPS structure
- **Problem-solving prompts**: "is there something you'd like to figure out at home? Let's brainstorm together"
- **No discipline-via-activity**: never use packets as a punishment or reward
- **Real choices**: kid has genuine agency in how the activity unfolds
- **Reflective prompts**: "what was hard about today?" "what would you change?"

## Sample parent-footer line

> 🌱 **A Ross-Greene note**: if [Kid] hits frustration with this, you might be looking at a *lagging skill*, not a behavior problem. Notice it without judgment; revisit in calm later.

## Activity types that fit especially well

Family meeting / problem-solving prompts · feelings check-in · concept-of-the-day · cooperative challenges · reflective journaling · "design a solution" engineering challenges

## What to avoid

- Reward charts
- Punishment language
- Shame framing
- "Just try harder" language
- Closed instructions that don't invite kid agency
