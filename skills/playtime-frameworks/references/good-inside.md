# Good Inside (Dr. Becky Kennedy)

**Core idea**: Every kid is "good inside" — behavior is the surface; underneath it is a feeling, a need, or a still-developing skill. The parent's job is to stay connected and curious through hard moments.

## Pillars

- **Most Generous Interpretation (MGI)**: assume the most generous explanation for behavior
- **Two things are true**: a kid can be having big feelings AND a limit can hold ("I see you really wanted that. We're done with screens for today.")
- **Sturdy leadership**: parent is the calm captain of the ship — not the kid's friend, not their enemy
- **Connection before correction**: tend the relationship first
- **Repair**: ruptures are normal; what matters is going back and naming them
- **The kid's job is to feel; ours is to hold**: emotions aren't problems to fix
- **Distinguish behavior from identity**: "you DID a hard thing" not "you ARE bad"

## Design implications for packets

- **Activities celebrate effort, not output.** Footer: "celebrate the trying, not the result."
- **Build in a "two things are true" moment** when relevant — e.g. mad libs that name a frustrating-AND-funny moment ("the playground was crowded AND we found the swings empty")
- **Emotion-coaching scripts** in the parent footer for predictably tender moments (transitions, mistakes, frustration with a hard puzzle)
- **No bribes, no shame language.** No "good job" / "bad job" — instead "you did a hard thing" or "you kept going"
- **Repair language** modeled when stories include a conflict
- **Kid-facing tone**: warm, sturdy, never mocking or saccharine
- **For meltdowns mid-activity**: the parent footer should include a tiny script: "Pause. Sit nearby. *'It's hard. I'm right here.'* Don't fix; just hold."

## Sample parent-footer line

> 💛 **A Good-Inside note**: if [Kid] gets stuck or frustrated mid-puzzle, you don't have to rush in. Try: *"Two things are true — this is hard, AND you've figured out hard things before. I'm right here."* Then wait. Sturdiness is the gift.

## Activity types that fit especially well

Feelings check-in · gratitude journal · acts of kindness · concept-of-the-day (esp. emotional virtues) · stories about big feelings · mindfulness · pretend play with conflict-resolution scenarios

## What to avoid

- Sticker-chart / external-reward scaffolding
- "Be a good helper!" framing
- Comparison to siblings or peers
- Failure framing
- Scripts that fix a feeling instead of welcoming it
