# Cooking recipe

## What it is

A kid-doable recipe with picture-led instructions, real ingredients, and a real edible result. Adult supervision flagged where needed (heat, knives, raw eggs).

## Age range

**3-12 years.**

## Skills it builds

- Sequencing
- Measuring (math!)
- Reading (older)
- Sensory: taste, texture, smell
- Patience (waiting for things to bake/rise)
- Kitchen confidence
- Cultural / family connection

## Format spec

A **single-page recipe** with:

- Title — kid-friendly + accurate ("Theo's One-Bowl Banana Muffins")
- **Yields** — how many it makes
- **Time** — prep + bake + total
- **Difficulty** — easy / medium / harder
- **Adult help needed for**: explicit list (turning oven on, hot pans, sharp knife, etc.)
- **Ingredients** — with picture icons next to each for under-7
- **Equipment** — bowls, spoons, measuring cups, etc.
- **Steps** — numbered, picture-iconned, kid-language
- **The taste-test moment** — built in
- **A question to end on** — "what would you change next time?"

## Quality bar

- **It actually works** — tested-recipe quality, not made-up. Use classics
- **Ingredient list = household-stocked** OR clearly notes "you'll need to grab" items
- **Safe** — heat / sharp / raw-egg moments are flagged
- **Kid-doable steps** — measure, pour, mix, sprinkle, mash, decorate
- **Adult-only steps** are minimal and labeled
- **Sensory framing** — "the dough should feel like ___; it'll smell like ___; you'll know it's done when ___"
- **Ends with eating** — that's the magic

## Variations

- **Family recipe** — interview a grandparent, document the recipe, cook it together
- **Recipe + science** — yeast + bread (biology), emulsion + dressing (chemistry)
- **Recipe + math** — double the recipe, halve it
- **Recipe + culture** — try a dish from another country and learn about it
- **Recipe + experiment** — "what happens if we use less sugar?"

## Material requirements

Per recipe. Always reference `household.materials` for kitchen-stocked basics. Highlight anything they'll need to grab.

## Print considerations

- Step-icons (mix, pour, measure, bake, decorate, eat)
- Big enough to follow with messy hands
- Lamination or "splatter zone" warning helpful
- One recipe per page (multi-page allowed for longer recipes)
- Color mode: color helps for "what it should look like"; B&W works with descriptive labels

## Example brief

> Kid: Theo, 6yr
> Interests: dragons, dinosaurs, eating
> Pantry: butter, flour, sugar, eggs, baking soda, chocolate chips, vanilla, salt

→ Title: "Theo's Dragon-Egg Cookies"
→ Yields: 12 cookies
→ Time: 15 min prep + 10 min bake = 25 min
→ Difficulty: easy
→ Adult help: turning oven on (350°F), opening hot oven
→ Ingredients (with icons):
  🥚 1 egg
  🧈 1/2 cup butter, softened
  🍯 1/2 cup brown sugar
  🍚 1 cup flour
  🧂 a pinch of salt
  🥄 1/2 tsp baking soda
  🍫 1 cup chocolate chips ("dragon eggs")
→ Equipment: 1 bowl, 1 wooden spoon, measuring cups, baking sheet, parchment
→ Steps (icons + number):
  1. Mush butter and sugar until creamy. (You can use your hands — wash them first!)
  2. Crack the egg in. Stir.
  3. Add flour, salt, and baking soda. Mix.
  4. Pour in the chocolate chips. Stir.
  5. Roll into balls, place on parchment paper.
  6. **Adult**: bake at 350°F for 9-11 minutes.
  7. Let them cool 5 minutes.
  8. Taste. Were they worth the wait?
→ Question: "What would you change next time? Crunchier? Bigger? More dragon eggs?"
