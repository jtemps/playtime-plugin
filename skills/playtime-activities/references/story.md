# Story (read-aloud)

## What it is

A short, original story written for **this** kid, on **this** theme, today. Read aloud or read independently. Optionally followed by 1-3 reflection questions.

## Age range

**All ages**, scaled to length and complexity:

| Age | Length | Sentences | Vocabulary |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0-2 yr | ~30 words | 4-6 short, rhythmic | Single-syllable, repetition |
| 2-3 yr | 60-100 words | 8-12, repetition + sound | Concrete nouns, simple verbs |
| 3-5 yr | 150-300 words | Full short story | Familiar + 1-2 stretch words |
| 5-8 yr | 300-600 words | Plot arc | Vocabulary stretch encouraged |
| 8-12 yr | 600-1500 words | Chapter / scene | Real literature voice |

## Skills it builds

- Vocabulary
- Listening comprehension
- Narrative structure
- Empathy + theory of mind
- Imagination

## Format spec

A **single-page (or two-page) story** with:

- A great title
- A clear narrative arc (even short ones: setup → trouble → resolution)
- One or two illustrations (described as SVG inline images, or simple illustrations sourced as inline-friendly art)
- Optional sidebar or footer with **3 conversation questions** for after reading
- For older kids: chapter-style structure with cliffhanger if part of a multi-day arc

## Quality bar

- The kid is **the protagonist** (or a very thinly-disguised version of them)
- Their **interests** show up — ocean kid → ocean story; dinosaur kid → dinosaur story
- The story has **real emotion** — not flat. A small problem, a real feeling, a real resolution
- **Not preachy** — no "and the moral of the story is..." — let the meaning live in the story
- **Beautiful sentences**: the story is written *well*, not phoned in. Read it aloud in your head; if it doesn't flow, rewrite
- **Right length** for the age — don't over-write; don't under-write
- **Avoid scary themes** unless cleared (check sensitivities); never include things on the no-fly list
- **Frameworks**: weave in framework-aligned moments naturally (Good Inside → emotion-naming; Hunt Gather → working alongside grown-up; Reggio → child-as-protagonist)

## Story shapes that work

- **The kid solves a problem** in their interest world ("Maya and the Megalodon's Missing Tooth")
- **A family member gets a story** (Grandma's coming → "The Day Grandma Brought the Sky")
- **A feeling story** — naming a feeling the kid had recently
- **A "first" story** — first day, first try, first mistake
- **A small adventure** — the dog gets out, a balloon escapes, snow comes early
- **A wonder story** — what's the moon doing tonight? Why do octopi have three hearts?

## Variations

- **Choose-your-own-adventure** (own reference file)
- **Story + drawing prompt** ("at the end, draw the cave they found")
- **Story + finish-the-story** ("now you write what happens next")
- **Story-in-parts** (a chapter a day, ~5-7 days for a full arc)
- **Bilingual** — alternate language sentences

## Material requirements

Printer. Optionally crayons for the drawing prompt.

## Print considerations

- Generous line spacing
- Serif font, 14-16pt for early readers, 12-14pt for older
- Optional illustration insets every ~150 words for younger kids
- Color mode: color illustrations if available, but text reads fine in B&W
- For non-readers: format as a script for the parent ("Read aloud — slowly")

## Reflection questions (3 max, optional)

- One **comprehension** ("what did [character] decide to do?")
- One **inference / empathy** ("how do you think [character] felt when ___?")
- One **personal connection** ("have you ever felt that?" / "what would you have done?")

## Example brief

> Kid: Theo, 5yr
> Interests: knights, dragons
> Theme: bravery
> Frameworks: Good Inside, Whole-Brain Child
> Sensitivities: scary monsters

→ Title: "Sir Theo and the Dragon Who Was Just Tired"
→ 280 words. A young knight discovers a dragon who isn't terrifying — just exhausted from carrying around big feelings. They sit together. The dragon names the feeling. They share a snack.
→ Three questions: "Why was the dragon roaring?" "How did Sir Theo feel when he first saw the dragon?" "What's a big feeling you carried this week?"
→ A small drawing prompt at the end: "Draw the dragon's snack."
